Representation

The way neurodivergent individuals are portrayed in stories – whether in novels, films, or TV shows – shapes how we think, speak, and act in real life. Popular culture can help build empathy and understanding, but it can also reinforce harmful stereotypes if we're not paying attention.

Sometimes, neurodivergent characters are shown as geniuses, burdens, or social outcasts – tropes that don't reflect the full humanity or diversity of real people's lives. Other times, they're included only for comic relief or dramatic tension, rather than as well-rounded individuals.

That's why positive and accurate representation of neurodiversity in media is so important. When stories reflect the richness of neurodivergent experiences – in school, at work, in friendships, and beyond – they help create a culture that is more inclusive, compassionate, and open-minded.

How Can We Examine Representations in Novels and Films?

You don't need to be an expert to start looking critically at stories. Here are a few simple steps to help you begin:

  1. Choose your story: Select a novel, film, or series where a character is described or implied to be neurodivergent (e.g., autistic, ADHD, dyslexic, etc.).
  2. Observe closely: Pay attention to how the character is introduced, treated by others, and how their neurodivergence is discussed (or not).
  3. Look beyond the plot: Ask yourself what the story wants you to feel about this character. Are they being respected or misunderstood? Are they shown with agency or always needing help?
  4. Reflect on the impact: Think about how this portrayal might affect someone watching or reading it – especially someone who is neurodivergent themselves.

Key Questions to Ask When Examining Representations

Asking these questions can help you critically evaluate how neurodiversity is presented in media:

  1. What words are used to describe the neurodivergent character? Are these respectful, accurate, or loaded with judgment?
  2. Does the story reduce the character to their diagnosis, or do they have a full personality, interests, and relationships?
  3. Is the character's neurodivergence portrayed as a problem to be fixed or a difference to be understood?
  4. How do teachers, peers, or family members interact with the character?
  5. Does the character's journey reinforce stereotypes (e.g., "the troubled genius" or "the antisocial recluse")?
  6. Does the story promote empathy and inclusion, or does it unintentionally reinforce fear or pity?
  7. Who tells the story – does the narrative include the neurodivergent character's own voice and perspective?

1 A Kind of Spark (Book) Author: Elle McNicoll
Publication Year: 2020
Synopsis: A middle-grade novel following Addie, an autistic girl who challenges societal misconceptions and uncovers hidden histories within her community. The narrative emphasizes her personal growth and advocacy for inclusion, offering a thoughtful portrayal of ND that resonates with young readers. 		Recommended for its authentic portrayal of an autistic protagonist and its challenge to common ND stereotypes. The book fosters awareness and empathy by presenting neurodiversity as a natural variation of human experience rather than a deficit or anomaly.
2 Simple Simon (Film) Release: 2010
Director: Andreas Öhman
Synopsis: A Swedish film that explores the life of an Asperger's boy living with his elder brother and the latter's girlfriend. When a conflict results in a dramatic upheaval in their household, the protagonist embarks on a mission to find a new partner for his brother. 		Provides a comparative framework by illustrating common narrative tropes in ND representation. Its similarities with later films (e.g., Aku Jati, Aku Asperger) make it a valuable resource for understanding how certain themes in portraying ND are sustained across cultures and time.
3 My Name Is Khan (Film) Director: Karan Johar
Release: 2010
Synopsis: Follows Rizwan Khan, a man with Asperger's syndrome, as he embarks on a transformative journey across America in the aftermath of the September 11 attacks. The film explores themes of prejudice, identity, and acceptance, offering a multifaceted portrayal of neurodiversity in a challenging socio-political context. 		Notable for its sensitive portrayal of neurodiversity, the film explores the intersection of disability, cultural identity, and social prejudice. Its narrative invites viewers to reflect on the importance of acceptance and resilience in the face of societal challenges.
4 Aku Jati, Aku Asperger (Film) Director: Fajar Bustomi
Release: 31 October 2024
Synopsis: Follows Jati, a boy with Asperger's syndrome, whose rigid routines and stereotypical portrayal spark public debate. The film has attracted attention on social media for its use of outdated terminology and a portrayal that simplifies complex ND experiences into easily digestible behavioral tropes. 		Serves as a case study in how stereotypical portrayals can oversimplify neurodivergent experiences, prompting discussions on the need for more authentic and nuanced representations in media.
5 Miracle in Cell No. 7 (Film) Director: Hanung Bramantyo
Release: 8 September 2022
Synopsis: A remake of a 2013 South Korean film, this movie tells the story of an autistic man wrongfully accused of rape and murder. Blending dramatic and comedic elements, it seeks to engage a broader audience while addressing themes of injustice and empathy. 		Demonstrates how genre adaptation (from drama to comedy) can influence public perceptions of neurodiversity and highlights the societal implications of wrongful accusations and empathy toward ND individuals.
