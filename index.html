What is Neurodiversity?

Neurodiversity is the range of differences in individual brain function and behavioral traits, regarded as part of normal variation in the human population.

Survey Result

51%

We conducted an online survey in September 2024 to see if people in Indonesia know about Neurodiversity. 51% of our respondents said that they never heard of it.

Some Typology

ASD

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neuro-developmental condition that affects how people interact with others, communicate, learn, and behave. It's called a "spectrum" disorder because it affects individuals differently and to varying degrees. The term encompasses a wide range of characteristics and levels of support needed. While the specific presentation of ASD varies greatly from person to person, there are some symptoms, particularly in young children, that people might more easily recognize such as social communication/interaction differences and repetitive or restricted behaviors or interests. However, a diagnosis of ASD requires a comprehensive evaluation by qualified professionals.

ADHD

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neuro-developmental condition characterized by persistent patterns of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interfere with functioning or development. These patterns are more frequent and severe than is typical for individuals at a comparable level of development. It's important to remember that everyone occasionally experiences difficulties with attention, hyperactivity, or impulsivity. However, in ADHD, these symptoms are chronic, pervasive (occurring in multiple settings like home, school, or work), and significantly impact daily life. Again, a comprehensive evaluation by a qualified healthcare professional is necessary to diagnose ADHD.

Dyslexia

Dyslexia is a specific learning disability that is neuro-biological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Essentially, dyslexia is a language-based learning disability that primarily affects reading. It's not about intelligence; people with dyslexia have a range of intellectual abilities. The core difficulty lies in processing the sounds of language (phonemes) and how they relate to written letters (graphemes).

Tourette Syndrome

Tourette Syndrome is a neuro-developmental disorder characterized by tics, which are sudden, repetitive, non-rhythmic movements or vocalizations. These tics are involuntary, meaning the person cannot easily control them. To be diagnosed with Tourette Syndrome, a person must have both multiple motor tics and at least one vocal tic at some point, although not necessarily at the same time. Tourette Syndrome is a complex condition, and its impact varies greatly from person to person. With understanding, support, and appropriate management strategies, individuals with TS can lead full and productive lives.

There are more conditions that fall under the umbrella of Neurodiversity such as Dyspraxia, Dysgraphia, Dyscalculia, Synesthesia, and other conditions. We only take four most common examples that people might associate those to Neurodiversity. It is important to note that Neurodiversity is not the same as Intellectual Disability. Neurodivergent Individual may very well be an intelligent person who has regular or high Intelligent Quotient (IQ).

Misconception

Why positive representation of Neurodiversity in media and our culture is important? because there are many mythologies out of our own ignorance.

Myth 1

“Neurodivergence is a disease that needs to be cured.”

Truth: Neurodivergence is a natural variation in brain wiring, not an illness. While support may be needed, the goal isn’t "fixing" but accommodating differences.

Myth 2

“All autistic people are geniuses or intellectually disabled.”

Truth: Autism (and neurodivergence generally) is a spectrum. Skills vary widely—some may excel in math or art, while others need support with daily tasks.

Myth 3

“People with ADHD are just lazy or undisciplined.”

Truth: ADHD involves executive dysfunction (e.g., focus, impulse control). With structure and tools, many thrive in dynamic careers (e.g., entrepreneurs, artists).

Myth 4

“Dyslexia means low intelligence.”

Truth: Dyslexia affects reading fluency, not IQ. Many dyslexics are gifted in problem-solving, creativity, or oral communication (e.g., Steven Spielberg, Whoopi Goldberg).

Myth 5

“Neurodivergent people lack empathy.”

Truth:They may express empathy differently (e.g., autistic people might struggle with social cues but feel deep compassion).

Myth 6

“Only boys are neurodivergent.”

Truth: Girls/women are underdiagnosed due to masking (copying social behaviors) or stereotypes (e.g., ADHD in girls labeled as "daydreaming")

Myth 7

“Neurodivergent people can’t succeed in school or careers.”

Truth: With accommodations (e.g., flexible deadlines, quiet workspaces), they excel. Many innovators (Daniel Radcliffe, Temple Grandin) are neurodivergent.

Myth 8

“Stimming (e.g., rocking, hand-flapping) is bad behavior.”

Truth: Stimming regulates emotions and sensory input. Suppressing it can cause distress.

Myth 9

“They don’t want friends—they prefer to be alone.”

Truth: Many crave connection but struggle with social norms. Inclusive environments help them build relationships.

Takeaways

Is neurodiversity a disability? No, it is not a disability. Rather, it represents the unique cognitive process on how a person understanding their world.

Here are some suggestions of what we should do regarding neurodiversity:

Understand

Understanding neurodivergent individuals goes beyond simply acknowledging their differences. It requires a genuine effort to learn about their unique ways of thinking, processing information, and experiencing the world. Take the initiative to learn about different neurodivergent conditions (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, Tourette's, etc.) and the diverse ways they can manifest. Be aware that each individual within a neurodivergent group is still unique. Understand that neurodivergence often comes with unique strengths and talents. Focus on identifying and appreciating these abilities rather than solely focusing on perceived deficits.There is no single "right" way to be. Embrace the diversity of human neurobiology and respect each individual's unique way of navigating the world.

Support

Providing support means creating environments and systems that are inclusive, accessible, and tailored to the needs of neurodivergent individuals. Be willing to make reasonable adjustments in various settings (educational, workplace, social) to support their needs. This might include providing quiet workspaces, visual aids, flexible deadlines, clear instructions, or alternative communication methods. Connect individuals with resources and tools that can help them thrive, such as assistive technologies, support groups, therapists specializing in neurodivergence, and educational materials. Actively work to remove barriers that might prevent neurodivergent individuals from accessing education, employment, social activities, and other opportunities.

Empower

It goes beyond general support and involves more active and personalized assistance to enable neurodivergent individuals to achieve their goals and participate fully in society. Create opportunities where their neurodivergent traits can be an asset. Empower neurodivergent individuals to understand their own needs and advocate for themselves effectively. Provide them with the tools and knowledge to do so. Acknowledge and celebrate their achievements, no matter how small they may seem. This reinforces positive growth and encourages continued progress.

Don’t we all need to be understood, supported, and empowered?

Best Practices

The language surrounding neurodiversity is continually evolving. Terms like "neurotypical" and "neurodivergent" have gained popularity in recent years, but there is ongoing debate about their accuracy and inclusivity.

Historically, neurodivergence has been associated with negative connotations and deficit-based language. Moving towards strengths-based and person-first language is crucial in promoting a positive and empowering view of neurodiversity.

Some individuals prefer to self-identify as neurodivergent, while others prefer specific diagnostic labels. Respecting individual preferences and avoiding assumptions about identity is essential.

Allowing the students to identify to whom they wish too. It could be that they do not wish to register with the Disability Team, but do want their tutors to know.

Examples of Best Practices in education:

  1. Implementing Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles helps create a flexible learning environment that accommodates diverse learning styles and needs. This includes offering multiple means of representation (e.g., visual, auditory, textual), engagement (e.g., choice, relevance, authenticity), and action and expression (e.g., varied response options, assistive technologies).

  2. Implement effective screening and support systems for early identification of neurodivergent learners.

  3. Recognizing that each neurodivergent student is unique, provide tailored accommodations and support plans based on individual strengths and challenges. This might involve adjusting assignments, providing assistive technology, or allowing for flexible deadlines (deadlines can also be non-negotiable as some students prefer these strict guidelines from their tutors to keep them on track).

  4. Develop and implement tailored plans to address the unique needs of each student.

  5. Focus on recognizing and building upon the unique strengths and talents of neurodivergent students rather than solely focusing on deficits. Encourage positive self-esteem and a sense of belonging.

  6. Provide educators with professional development on neurodiversity and inclusive teaching practices.
