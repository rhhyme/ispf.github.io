About Us

Funded by The British Council ISPF (International Science Partnerships Fund), Anglia Ruskin University (ARU)–United Kingdom– and Sampoerna University–Indonesia– embarked on this knowledge exchange project aiming at mainstreaming and capacity building in neurodiverse-inclusive education (NIE) in HE.

Introduction

Having previously enlisted in the group of lower middle-income (LM) countries in 2022-2023, Indonesia now moves to the upper middle-income (UM) group in the Development Assistant Committee (DAC) list of ODA recipients in 2024-2025. The next review will be in 2026. The DAC considers the UM countries are those with a Gross National Income (GNI) per capita between USD 4,096 and USD 12,695, whilst LM countries have a GNI per capita between USD 1,046 and USD 4,095 [OECD.org, DAC 2022-2023 list]. World Bank reported Indonesia's GNI per capita increases from USD 4070 in 2019 to USD 4580, despite a drop of GNI to USD3900 in 2020. Although the country is on an upward trajectory, the country still faces significant challenges in poverty reduction, provision of social services, infrastructure development and tackling corruption, amongst others.

One of the strategies for international development (IDS) of the UK foreign policy is on the development of engagement with Indonesia, which focuses on green growth, human capital development and trade & investment. The IDS Roadmap considers sectors with the greatest common interests between the two nations, for instance: the cross-cutting technology, and sustainable economic and human capital development. The UK pledged to provide strong technical expertise that will be of value to Indonesia. In human capital development, there is an ambition to support the delivery of Indonesia's own human development priorities in an equitable and inclusive way, through the provision of UK expertise at both institutional and community levels, helping to strengthen Indonesian government, civil society, cultural, education and democratic institutions [FCDO, Policy Paper July 2023]

To this date, parts of the ODA focus on the provision of technical expertise and access to UK institutions, therefore access for marginalised groups (including disabilities, women/girls) to modernised public services and education [OECD.org]. The investment is also directed to ensure that marginalised people can influence and access the benefits of change, including challenging stereotypes to take advantage of skills development and opportunities to engage with new and emerging technologies crucial to a successful economy. This is very much aligned with the Indonesia's 1945 constitution, Article 27: stating that every Indonesian citizen must have equal access to a job opportunity. The right for the job applies to all Indonesian people, including people with disabilities. Article 1, point (1) Law Number 8 Year 2016 regarding people with disabilities states that people with disabilities refer to a condition with physical, intellectual, mental or sensory limitations for an extended period of time, and this limitation gives difficulties when interacting with others. This article emphasizes that people with disabilities have the same rights without any discrimination in any aspect [peraturan go.id].

In Indonesia, ~30% of school-aged children with disabilities do not have access to education, and many of those who are in school are underserved. A pilot project run by Wahana Inklusif Indonesia Foundation, took place in Lebak, Cilacap, Bondowoso, Ponorogo, and Bima districts, from October 2021 to November 2022 was to develop a holistic evaluation method to assess children with learning disabilities in their learning of Bahasa (Indonesian Language) and Mathematics. This project was initiated in response to a request from the Ministry of Education, Culture (MoEC) to assess students with disabilities during the COVID-19 pandemic. As a result, more than 100 students with learning disabilities received a thorough diagnoses of their learning needs for the first time. Teachers received support in assessing reading and mathematics ability and in gathering health and psychological data for educators (mentors), psychologists, and health experts to assess students' progress [The World Bank, Feb.2024]. Subsequently, by 2030, Indonesia will need at least 113 million skilled workers, particularly with technical ability. With the Indonesia population nearing 280million, the Government realised the country's human capital potential. They have invested in Higher Education, including polytechnic schools to produce more skilled engineers.

The proposed project leveraging international collaboration between ARU and Sampoerna provides an opportunity to foster a more collaborative and interconnected inclusive-educational landscape, within the higher education (HE) context. This facilitates a two-way learning environment and exchange of knowledge between the two countries and advancing provision of neurodiverse-inclusive education (NIE) within subjects/courses' curriculum (modules or syllabi).

This potentially will transform the teaching and learning (T&L) which extends to assessment and feedback in HE, which will impact neurodivergent students and staff. This proposed project will address the inequality challenges for the marginalised groups, the unemployment (societal challenges), and the needs of skilled professionals. The proposed goal of this project is aligned with the DAC-ODA roadmap and will support the Indonesia's Government and the UK foreign policy's international development strategy.

Mission

Leveraging interdisciplinary research in social science, pedagogy for NIE with the main focus in the engineering education, we are developing a Knowledge Base consisting of methodologies to recognise neurodiversity, best practices derived from national and international policies, and strategies for tailoring curriculum/syllabus for NIE.

Objective

  • Knowledge Exchange in pedagogy for Neurodiversity in Higher Education (HE)
  • Best Practice development following qualitative study on existing policies, including those of Governments, own and partners' institutions
  • Model syllabi/curriculums comprising NIE inclusion; such as for mechanical engineering modules (e.g., design, structure).
  • Develop an "International Research and Network in Neurodiverse-Inclusive Education" (IRNNIE) Group.

Documentation Report

The ISPF team’s visit to ARU represents a significant step in the collaborative efforts between ARU and Sampoerna University (SU), fully funded by the British Council

 

The SU team’s visit to the UK – exploring ARU campuses in Chelmsford and Cambridge as well as meetings and workshops with lecturers, students, and staff – has been a transformative experience. These engagements have deepened our understanding of neurodiversity and its integration into Higher Education, offering practical insights into creating learning environments that are inclusive and supportive of neurodivergent students.

This research collaboration has highlighted the critical importance of awareness, institutional readiness, and community support in creating educational systems that accommodate diverse needs. As we move forward, our shared goal is to challenge stigma, bridge gaps, and cultivate an inclusive culture that supports neurodivergent individuals in achieving their full potential

Anglia Ruskin University
November 11-15, 2024

Students at Anglia Ruskin University Chelmsford Visiting Our Booth During Public Engagement Session

ISPF Workshop Day, Helmore Building, ARU Cambridge, Tuesday, November 12, 2024. We shared our knowledge and perspective regarding neurodiversity, especially in higher education context.

On Thursday, 14 November 2024, the ISPF team from both Sampoerna University and Anglia Ruskin University held a public engagement session at the ARU Chelmsford campus. We set up a booth in the lobby of the Lord Ashcroft Building, featuring information leaflets about our joint project, interactive neurodiversity-themed games, and fun freebies like squishy balls, colourful lenses to simulate dyslexic reading, and a magnetic LEGO tiles.

As students passed by between classes, many stopped to chat. Some shared personal stories about neurodivergence in their families, while others opened up about their own experiences navigating university as neurodivergent students. Many were simply curious about the project, about neurodiversity, or even about Indonesia, especially after sampling some Indonesian treats we brought along!

It was a vibrant afternoon of sharing and learning, and it reminds us how powerful small conversations can be in building awareness and empathy.
